title: "Choosing an Assayer and Practical Buyer Guidance"
description: "Practical, sensible guidance on choosing a trustworthy assayer, covering accreditation, independence, documentation, and other simple checks anyone can apply before a precious metal transaction."
---

For anyone new to buying or selling gold, silver, or other precious metals, deciding who should test a sample can feel like an unfamiliar and slightly daunting task. Fortunately, the underlying principles involved are straightforward once explained clearly, and applying a few sensible practices can make this process far less intimidating than it might first appear. This guidance focuses on the practical steps anyone can take when approaching the selection of an assayer, regardless of how much prior experience they might have with precious metals or how large or small the transaction in question happens to be.

The first consideration involves understanding what qualifications and accreditation genuinely mean within this field. A properly accredited laboratory or individual assayer has typically been assessed by a recognised professional body against established standards covering equipment, procedure, staff training, and ongoing performance. Asking directly whether an assayer holds current accreditation, and from which body, is a perfectly reasonable question that any legitimate professional should answer clearly and without hesitation. If this question produces vague or evasive responses, that alone is worth treating as a meaningful signal, since a genuinely accredited assayer has nothing to be defensive about and a fraudster operating without proper credentials often relies precisely on nobody thinking to ask.

Independence forms another central pillar of sensible assayer selection. As discussed elsewhere, an assayer with no financial or personal connection to either party in a transaction provides results that both sides can trust without needing to rely on each other's honesty. Before selecting an assayer, it is worth asking plainly whether they have any existing relationship with the other party involved in the transaction. A straightforward, transparent answer to this question is a good sign. Reluctance to answer, or an answer that raises further questions, deserves further [investigation before proceeding](https://closecallcases.org/podcast/fraud-case-study-podcasts-index.php).

Cost is naturally a practical consideration, though it should not be the only factor weighed when selecting an assayer. Reasonable fees for testing vary depending on the type of material, the method used, and the complexity of the work involved, but dramatically low prices compared to typical market rates can sometimes indicate corners being cut on procedure or accreditation, or in less common cases, a get rich quick arrangement where the unusually low fee is simply bait designed to win business before a far less honest pattern of behaviour reveals itself later. Equally, an unusually high price does not automatically guarantee superior quality. Comparing quotes and services from several different laboratories before committing to one provides a useful sense of what reasonable pricing looks like for the specific type of testing required.

There have been documented situations where buyers, eager to complete a transaction quickly, accepted results from an assayer offering unusually favourable terms without first checking basic credentials or independence. In several of these cases, the promised value of the precious metal in question turned out to be far lower than reported, with the buyer left struggling afterwards to recover any of the money already paid. These situations serve as a useful reminder that a result obtained too easily, too quickly, or too cheaply compared to what would normally be expected deserves a moment of extra scrutiny rather than simple relief at having found a convenient solution.

Turnaround time matters as well, though it should be balanced against thoroughness rather than treated as the most important factor on its own. Reputable assayers generally provide a realistic estimate of how long testing will take, based on the method involved and their current workload, and are willing to explain this timeline clearly. Extreme urgency presented as a requirement, particularly when combined with pressure to skip normal verification steps, can sometimes indicate an attempt to rush a transaction past the point where proper scrutiny would otherwise occur.

Documentation deserves particular attention when choosing an assayer. A proper testing report should clearly state what method was used, what result was obtained, and ideally include enough detail that the process could be understood and, if necessary, repeated by another laboratory. Reports that simply state a final figure without any supporting detail provide far less confidence than a properly documented result, since the absence of detail makes it much harder to identify errors or question the methodology used if something later seems inconsistent with other available information. A buyer or seller receiving only a brief, unexplained number should feel entirely comfortable asking for greater detail, and a reasonable assayer will generally welcome the opportunity to provide it rather than treating the request as an imposition.

It is also worth thinking about how an assayer is found in the first place, since the route taken to locate a professional can itself carry useful information. Recommendations from established, reputable organisations within the precious metals trade tend to carry more weight than a name suggested only by the other party to the transaction, particularly where that other party has an obvious interest in steering testing towards someone they trust to produce a favourable outcome. Where possible, sourcing a shortlist of potential assayers independently, rather than relying entirely on whoever the seller happens to suggest, provides a useful additional layer of confidence in the eventual choice.

It is also worth considering the physical and operational standing of a laboratory before relying heavily on its results for a significant transaction. A laboratory with appropriate, well maintained equipment, a track record of consistent results over time, and a willingness to discuss its general procedures openly tends to inspire far more confidence than one that seems disorganised, secretive, or unable to answer basic questions about how testing is actually carried out on the premises.

For particularly significant transactions, seeking a second opinion from an entirely separate, unconnected laboratory remains one of the strongest practical safeguards available to any buyer or seller. While this involves additional time and modest extra cost, the protection it provides against the rare but real risk of corrupted or mistaken results is often well worth the investment, particularly when the sums involved in the underlying transaction are substantial enough that an inaccurate figure could represent a significant financial loss.

Communication throughout the testing process also deserves attention. Both parties to a transaction should ideally have equal access to the assayer, equal opportunity to ask questions, and equal access to the final report once it has been produced. An arrangement where only one party communicates with the assayer, while the other is simply handed a result after the fact, introduces an imbalance that can undermine confidence in the overall process, even where nothing has actually gone wrong.

It is worth remembering throughout this process that the overwhelming majority of assayers and laboratories operate honestly and competently, providing a genuinely valuable service that supports confidence throughout the precious metals trade. The guidance offered here is not intended to suggest that suspicion should be the default approach to every interaction. Rather, understanding what good practice looks like allows anyone to recognise when something falls short of that standard, providing a sensible basis for asking further questions or seeking additional verification without assuming the worst about every assayer encountered along the way.

Building a simple checklist before approaching any significant precious metal transaction can help keep these considerations organised. Confirming accreditation, establishing independence, comparing reasonable pricing and turnaround times across several options, insisting on properly documented results, and considering a second opinion for particularly significant sums together form a practical, manageable approach that does not require any specialist scientific background to apply effectively. These steps, taken together, provide a solid foundation for confidently navigating the process of selecting an assayer, whatever level of experience someone brings to their first or their fiftieth precious metal transaction.

It is also worth keeping a simple record of the entire process once a transaction is complete, including copies of any communication with the assayer, the final report, and details of how payment for the service itself was arranged. This kind of record rarely needs to be referred to again, but in the relatively rare event that a question or dispute does arise later, having a clear, organised record makes resolving the matter far simpler than trying to reconstruct events from memory or scattered correspondence. A buyer or seller who later discovers they have been the victim of a deliberately misleading result will find this kind of documentation invaluable, both for pursuing any available legal remedy and for warning others within their own professional or social circle about the specific pattern of deceit they encountered.

Ultimately, choosing an assayer well comes down to applying ordinary good sense, the same kind of careful consideration anyone would naturally bring to any significant financial decision. There is nothing mysterious or overly technical about the core principles involved. Confirming proper qualifications, insisting on genuine independence, expecting clear documentation, and remaining willing to seek a second opinion when the stakes justify it together provide more than enough protection for the vast majority of situations anyone is likely to encounter within the precious metals trade, allowing confidence to rest on solid, verifiable foundations rather than on hope alone.
