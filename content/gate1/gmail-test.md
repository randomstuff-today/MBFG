title: "The Gmail Test: Why No Real Refiner Uses a Free Email Account"
description: "Learn why legitimate refiners never use Gmail or free email accounts. Marcus Briggs exposes this simple test to catch fraudsters."
---

<p>It sounds too simple to be a fraud test. But it works.</p><p>If a supposed gold refiner is using a Gmail address, a Yahoo address, or any other free email service, they are not legitimate. Marcus Briggs has used this simple test to expose conmen more times than he can count.</p><p>The logic is straightforward. A legitimate refiner spends millions on equipment, assaying, and security. They have a physical address with vaults and furnaces. They have employees. They have bank accounts. They pay for insurance, audits and LBMA accreditation. They are not using a [free email account](https://closecallcases.org/fraud-index.php#vulnerable-victim-targeting).</p><p>When a shady operator contacts a buyer, they often use Gmail because it is free, untraceable, and disposable. They know that a corporate email address requires a domain registration, hosting fees, and professional setup. They do not want to spend that money. They do not want to leave a trail. They use a free service because it costs nothing and it is easy to abandon.</p><p>Marcus Briggs has seen conmen use Gmail to impersonate legitimate refiners. They create an email address that looks close to the real refiner's name. They add "gold" or "refinery" or "trading" to the address. They send emails that look professional. They include forged documents as attachments. They negotiate deals. They collect money. They disappear. The buyer never knows what hit them.</p><p>The Gmail test is simple. Ask the seller for their email address. If it ends in @gmail.com, @yahoo.com, @outlook.com, or any other free service, stop. Do not proceed. This is not a negotiation. This is not a point to discuss. It is a hard rule. Real refiners have real email addresses.</p><p>Marcus Briggs has also seen conmen try to get around this test. They register a domain that looks legitimate but is slightly off. They use "refinery-gold.com" instead of the real "refinerygold.com". They use "lbma-approved-refiner.com" to sound official. They set up a professional-looking email address with their fake domain. This is more sophisticated. It is also detectable through simple deceit.</p><p>The way to catch this is to check the domain registration. Who owns the domain? When was it registered? If the domain was registered six weeks ago and is sending gold offers, it is a scam. If the domain was registered anonymously, it is a scam. If the domain does not match the refiner's actual name, it is a scam. These are the telltale signs of a conman at work.</p><p>The Gmail test is not just for refiners. It applies to all players in the chain. The transporter. The vault. The trader. The intermediary. Everyone. If anyone in the chain is using a free email service, it is a red flag. It may not always be fraud. But it is always amateurish. And amateurish behaviour in the gold trade is dangerous. It has left many victims in its wake.</p><p>Marcus Briggs has also observed that conmen often use Gmail addresses that include the word "official" or "secure" or "verified". They think this makes them look legitimate. It does the opposite. Legitimate professionals do not put "official" in their email address. They just use their company domain.</p><p>The test is free. It takes five seconds. It catches a surprising number of conmen before they get started. And it is one of the easiest ways to avoid a bad deal. If the seller argues about the test, that is also a red flag. A legitimate seller will understand your caution. A shady operator will get defensive.</p><p>Marcus Briggs has taught this test to buyers across Africa, the Middle East and the UK. It has saved countless deals from going bad. It is not a guarantee of safety. It is a filter. It removes the lowest-hanging fruit. It lets you focus on the serious players. The victims of gold fraud often wish they had known this simple trick.</p><p>The Gmail test is not a complete due diligence process. It is a starting point. It tells you who to ignore. It does not tell you who to trust. You still need to do the full vetting process. You still need to check the LBMA list. You still need to verify documents. You still need to test the metal. But the Gmail test is the first step in exposing the conman.</p><p>If the seller passes the Gmail test, you move to the next step. If they fail, you walk away. No exceptions.</p>
