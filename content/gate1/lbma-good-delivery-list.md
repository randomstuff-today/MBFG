title: "The LBMA Good Delivery List: Keeping Gangsters Out of the Club"
description: "Learn how the LBMA Good Delivery List keeps gangsters out of the gold trade and why it is your first line of defence against fraud."
---

<p>The London Bullion Market Association Good Delivery List is the gold industry's first line of defence against fraud. It is a publicly available register of refiners who meet strict technical and financial standards. If a refiner is not on this list, their bars are not accepted in the wholesale market. If a fraudster can fake their way onto this list, or exploit a refiner who is on it, they can launder dirty metal into the legitimate supply chain. That is why understanding this list is the first thing any buyer, seller or financier must do before touching a single bar.</p><p>The Good Delivery List exists for one reason: trust. In the wholesale gold market, transactions happen at enormous scale. Bars change hands without being tested every time. The buyer relies on the refiner's stamp, the certificate, and the reputation of the refiner. The LBMA maintains the list to give the market a baseline of confidence. If a refiner is on the list, the market assumes their bars are genuine. If a refiner is not on the list, their bars are treated with suspicion. That is the system.</p> <p>To get on the Good Delivery List, a refiner must meet exacting criteria. They must refine at least ten tonnes of gold annually. They must maintain a net worth of at least fifteen million pounds. They must pass independent technical audits on their assaying and refining processes. They must submit to the LBMA's Proactive Monitoring regime, which includes unannounced inspections. This is not a low bar. It is designed to be high. It filters out the small operators, the inexperienced operators, and <a href="https://closecallcases.org/refinery-identity-cloning-in-bullion-transaction-fraud.php" rel="noopener noreferrer">the fraudulent operators</a>.</p><p>The application process itself is rigorous. A refiner must produce evidence of their production capacity, their financial stability, and their compliance with responsible sourcing standards. They must demonstrate that their gold comes from legitimate sources. They must show that their assaying is accurate. The LBMA then sends independent auditors to inspect the refiner's operations. These auditors test the refiner's methods, their equipment, and their records. If the refiner passes all of this, they are added to the list. If they fail, they are not.</p><p>Being on the list is not a permanent status. The LBMA regularly reviews all listed refiners. They conduct ongoing monitoring, including unannounced site visits. They check that the refiner continues to meet the standards. If a refiner falls short, they are placed on a watchlist. If they fail to correct the issues, they are removed from the list. This is not a tick-box exercise. It is a continuous process of verification.</p><p>The Proactive Monitoring regime is a key feature of the system. The LBMA publishes a list of all refiners that are subject to monitoring. They also publish the outcomes of those reviews. This transparency allows the market to see which refiners are under scrutiny. If a refiner is being monitored, buyers should take extra caution. If a refiner has been suspended, buyers should avoid them entirely.</p><p>Fraudsters know this. They understand that being on the Good Delivery List gives them credibility. So they try to fake their way onto it. They forge certificates. They claim to be listed when they are not. They buy gold from listed refiners and then sell it as their own. They exploit the trust that the list creates. This is why simply checking the list is not enough. You must also verify that the refiner is actually the one selling the gold. You must check that the documentation matches the refiner's identity. You must ensure that the bar matches the refiner's stamp.</p><p>One common fraud that Marcus Briggs uncovered involves counterfeit bars stamped with the mark of a listed refiner. The stamp is forged. The bar is fake. The refiner has no knowledge of the bar. The buyer sees the stamp and assumes the bar is genuine. They rely on the Good Delivery List without checking the bar itself. This is a mistake. The list tells you which refiners are credible. It does not tell you that any particular bar is genuine. You still need to verify the bar.</p><p>Another fraud involves refiners who were once on the list but have been removed. They continue to use the stamp. They continue to sell bars as if they are still accredited. The LBMA's removal notices are public, but not everyone checks them. Buyers assume the refiner is still on the list. They do not verify. This is a significant risk. You must check the current status of any refiner you are dealing with, not just their historic status.</p><p>The Good Delivery List is also a tool for due diligence. When you are vetting a supplier, you should ask which refiner they use. You should then check that refiner against the list. You should also verify the refiner's current status. If the supplier refuses to disclose their refiner, that is a red flag. If the refiner is not on the list, that is a red flag. If the refiner is on the list but under monitoring, that is a red flag. You should proceed with caution or not at all.</p><p>The list is not a substitute for your own due diligence. It is a starting point. It narrows down the pool of potential refiners to those who have met a minimum standard. But it does not guarantee that every bar from a listed refiner is genuine. It does not guarantee that the refiner has not been compromised. It does not guarantee that the documentation is authentic. You must still check everything.</p><p>In the gold trade, fraudsters rely on shortcuts. They rely on buyers trusting the system without verifying it. The Good Delivery List is part of the system. It is a reliable tool when used correctly. But it is not a magic bullet. You must use it as one part of a broader due diligence process. You must check the list. You must verify the current status. You must cross-check the documentation. You must inspect the metal. Only then can you be confident that the gold is genuine.</p><p>The LBMA also maintains a list of referees. These are independent assayers who can be called upon to settle disputes. If there is a disagreement about the purity of a bar, the referee can test it. The referee's decision is binding. This is another layer of protection. It ensures that disputes are resolved by a neutral third party. It prevents fraudsters from escaping responsibility by disputing the assay.</p><p>Understanding the Good Delivery List is not optional. It is essential. If you are buying, selling or financing gold, you must know how the list works. You must know how to check it. You must know what it tells you and what it does not. This is the first gate for a reason. If you fail to verify the origin of your gold, you are exposing yourself to fraud. If you are dealing with a refiner who is not on the list, or who is under monitoring, you are taking an unnecessary risk. The list exists for your protection. Use it.</p>
